## SECTION 2: THE 5 AXES

The spider chart plots five dimensions on a 1–10 scale. Each axis is scored from answers to 3 specific questions. The shape of the pentagon is what makes every report feel personal — no two people score identically across all five.

**1. Deal Instinct (DI)**
How naturally wired you are to find, evaluate, and pursue business acquisition opportunities. High scorers see deals everywhere and move toward them instinctively. Low scorers are unfamiliar with the process or mentally distant from it. This is the most learnable axis — but only if you deliberately build it.

**2. Operator Depth (OD)**
Your ability and appetite to run a business hands-on. High scorers have operational intuition, aren't afraid of complexity, and know how to build and manage teams through uncertainty. Low scorers are earlier in the ownership journey or more comfortable as investors than operators.

**3. Capital Readiness (CR)**
Your current financial position and sophistication around deal financing. High scorers are capital-ready and understand how leverage works in deal structures. Low scorers need more time, capital accumulation, or education about how acquisitions are actually financed before they can act.

**4. Risk Tolerance (RT)**
Your psychological and practical relationship with uncertainty. High scorers make decisions with incomplete information and hold steady when things get hard. Low scorers need more certainty before moving and may struggle through the inevitable rough patches of ownership.

**5. Strategic Vision (SV)**
Your ability to think long-term about wealth, ownership, and what you're actually building. High scorers have a clear picture of where they're going and make deal decisions in service of that. Low scorers are more reactive and near-term focused.

## SECTION 4: SCORING & ARCHETYPE LOGIC

### Axis Score Calculation

Each axis score = average of 3 question scores (Q scores: 2, 4, 7, or 9). Round to one decimal.

**Score Ranges:**
| Range | Label |
|---|---|
| 1.0 – 3.5 | Low |
| 3.6 – 6.5 | Medium |
| 6.6 – 10.0 | High |

---

### Archetype Score Formulas

Each archetype is calculated using a weighted combination of axis scores. The highest archetype score = primary archetype. The second highest = secondary archetype (referenced in report personalization).

| Archetype | Formula |
|---|---|
| The Acquirer | (DI × 0.40) + (SV × 0.35) + (RT × 0.25) |
| The Operator | (OD × 0.40) + (DI × 0.35) + (RT × 0.25) |
| The Builder | (OD × 0.40) + (SV × 0.35) + (CR × 0.25) |
| The Architect | (SV × 0.40) + (CR × 0.35) + (OD × 0.25) |

---

### The Four Archetypes — Identity Descriptors

**The Acquirer**
*You're wired for deals. You don't just want to own a business — you want to find the right one, structure it intelligently, and build a portfolio over time. You think from the deal side first.*

**The Operator**
*You want to own a business and actually run it. Not from a spreadsheet — in it. You believe the gap between a mediocre business and a great one is almost always an operations problem, and you know how to close that gap.*

**The Builder**
*You've already done something hard — you own a business and you're running it. Now you're focused on making it bigger, better, and less dependent on you. You're not done building.*

**The Architect**
*You think in systems. Not just how a single business works, but how multiple assets, income streams, and equity positions fit together into something that generates wealth without requiring your constant attention. You're playing a long game.*

## SECTION 5: SPIDER CHART SPECIFICATIONS

**Chart Type:** Radar / Spider Chart
**Axes:** 5 (Deal Instinct, Operator Depth, Capital Readiness, Risk Tolerance, Strategic Vision)
**Scale:** 1–10 per axis
**Rendering:** Filled polygon (user's shape) overlaid on a faint outer benchmark ring

**Benchmark Ring:** Set at 8.0 on all axes — not 10. A perfect 10 on everything isn't realistic or relatable. The gap between the user's shape and the benchmark ring should feel meaningful but achievable, not discouraging.

**Visual callout near the chart:**
*"No two profiles look the same. Your shape tells the story."*

**Color guidance:**
- User polygon: Deep fill in brand color (dark teal or similar)
- Benchmark ring: Light gray, no fill
- Axis labels: Bold at each vertex with axis name and score shown (e.g., "Deal Instinct: 7.3")

**Psychological function of the chart:** The spider chart creates the perception of a complex, personalized output that feels impossible to fake. Even two people with the same primary archetype will have visibly different shapes. This is the visual that gets screenshotted and shared.

### 7.1 DEAL INSTINCT

---

**LOW (1.0 – 3.5)**

*Variation A*
Right now, deal sourcing isn't a muscle you've built yet — and that's more common than you'd think. Most people who end up buying a business didn't start out thinking like a deal person. What changes is exposure. The more time you spend around transactions, the more you start to see businesses as opportunities rather than just places you interact with. The good news: this is the most learnable of the five dimensions. It builds fast once you decide to pay attention.

*Variation B*
You're not yet in the habit of looking at the world through an acquisition lens. That's not a knock — it just means you're earlier in the journey than you might be in other areas. Deal instinct isn't innate talent; it's pattern recognition that builds through reps. People who score high here usually spent years around businesses, watched transactions happen up close, or made a deliberate decision to start paying attention. You can shortcut that timeline significantly by surrounding yourself with people already in active deal flow.

*Variation C*
The deal-finding side of business acquisition is still largely unfamiliar territory for you. Most people in your position know they want to own something but don't yet have a feel for where deals come from or how to spot a good one. The honest truth is that this knowledge is teachable — and it's one of the main things separating people who think about buying a business from people who actually do it. Building this muscle is probably your highest-leverage first move.

*Variation D*
When it comes to finding and evaluating business opportunities, you're at the starting line. That's not unusual — the deal world has its own vocabulary, its own channels, and its own logic that most people were never taught. What's worth knowing is that deal instinct is built, not born. A year from now, if you're intentional about it, you'll look back at your current score and barely recognize it. The question is whether you're ready to start building.

---

**MEDIUM (3.6 – 6.5)**

*Variation A*
You have a real baseline of deal awareness. You notice businesses. You've probably thought about what you'd do if you owned a few of the ones you interact with regularly. The gap between where you are and where high-deal-instinct acquirers operate isn't one of talent — it's reps and process. You likely just need a structured approach to deal review, a consistent source of deal flow, and a few more reps evaluating real businesses before your instinct sharpens into something you can act on confidently.

*Variation B*
There's genuine deal curiosity here. You've started thinking like a buyer even if you haven't completed the full journey yet. Where people at your score level tend to get stuck is in moving from "interested in deals" to "active in deals" — meaning the leap from browsing to actually submitting an LOI feels large. Part of what closes that gap is process: knowing what to look for in a P&L, having a clear checklist, and understanding what questions to ask a seller. That process is entirely learnable.

*Variation C*
You're somewhere in the middle of building your acquisition instinct — past the starting line but not yet operating like someone for whom deal evaluation is second nature. That's actually a useful place to be. You've got enough exposure to know you're interested and to ask the right questions. What sharpens this next is volume: reviewing more deals, even ones you'll never buy, builds the pattern recognition that makes good deals obvious faster. Most people underestimate how dramatically this accelerates with deliberate repetition.

*Variation D*
Your deal sense is developing. You think about businesses with more of a buyer's eye than most people, but you haven't fully operationalized that instinct yet. The interesting thing about where you sit is that you're close — closer than the score might suggest. For a lot of people at this level, one solid acquisition education experience or the right community of active buyers is enough to close the gap. You already have the curiosity. The missing piece is probably structure and deal access.

**HIGH (6.6 – 10.0)**

*Variation A*
Deal instinct is clearly a strength. You notice opportunities in the businesses around you, you understand how to evaluate what you're looking at, and you're not intimidated by the deal process. This is one of the harder dimensions to develop from scratch, so the fact that it comes naturally puts you meaningfully ahead of most aspiring acquirers. The risk at this level is overconfidence — moving too fast on a deal before doing full diligence. Trust your instinct, but verify it.

*Variation B*
You think like a deal person. You see businesses everywhere and your brain naturally goes to the question of what they're worth and whether you'd want to own them. That's a genuinely rare trait — most people never develop this frame regardless of how much education they get. The main thing to watch: high deal instinct can lead to a pipeline that gets too wide, too fast. Constraint and selectivity are the disciplines that channel this strength productively.

*Variation C*
Acquisition thinking comes naturally to you. You've built the mental habit of evaluating businesses, and you understand enough about deal mechanics to move quickly when you find something worth moving on. What separates people at your deal instinct level who close deals from those who stay in perpetual search mode is decisiveness. The instinct is there. The question is whether you've matched it with enough conviction to pull the trigger when the right one shows up.

*Variation D*
Your deal instinct is in the top tier. You've done the reps — whether in your career, through past research, or just through years of paying attention — and it shows. You evaluate businesses quickly, you know what you're looking for, and you have a sense for when something is worth pursuing. At this level, the edge you need isn't more deal education. It's access: better deal flow, a stronger network of sellers and brokers, and the infrastructure to close efficiently when you find the right target.

### 7.2 OPERATOR DEPTH

---

**LOW (1.0 – 3.5)**

*Variation A*
Operations isn't where your instincts currently live — and that's worth being honest about going in. Business ownership, at least in the early stages, requires you to touch a lot of functions you may never have managed before. Vendors, employees, local marketing, customer complaints, cash flow timing — none of this waits. None of it is a reason to avoid ownership, but it does mean your first acquisition should probably be a business with a strong existing team, not a fixer-upper that needs an operator from day one.

*Variation B*
Your operational experience is still developing. That matters in acquisition because the gap between owning a business on paper and running one on Monday morning is almost always an operational one. People with lower operator depth tend to do best when they focus their first acquisition on a stable, well-run business where their job is stewardship, not rebuilding. If you find yourself drawn to distressed opportunities or turnaround situations, be honest that you'll need a strong operator on the team before you can go there.

*Variation C*
Operations isn't a strength you're leaning on yet. For the right acquisition, that's manageable — plenty of people have built significant business wealth without being gifted operators themselves. But you'll need to compensate somewhere: either by acquiring businesses that already have strong management in place, by being willing to pay a premium for turnkey operations, or by partnering with an operator from the outset. Going in underprepared on the operational side is one of the most common reasons first acquisitions struggle.

*Variation D*
On the operational dimension, you're still building. Not every successful business owner is a natural operator, and frankly, some of the best acquirers aren't. But you should go in with both eyes open. The businesses that punish low operational depth hardest are ones with thin teams, owner-dependent processes, and deferred problems. The businesses most forgiving of it are those with established systems, tenured staff, and clear SOPs. That's where you should be looking first.

---

**MEDIUM (3.6 – 6.5)**

*Variation A*
You have a functional understanding of operations and can navigate complexity when you need to, even if it's not where you naturally thrive. That's a solid foundation. Most business acquirers aren't operators first — they're learners who figure it out. Where you'll want to be deliberate is in identifying which operational areas you're genuinely weak in before you buy, and either building a team around those gaps or finding a business that doesn't expose them immediately.

*Variation B*
You're reasonably capable operationally, which is enough to be a successful owner if you're thoughtful about what you buy. The honest truth is that most small business acquisition success stories don't require elite operational talent — they require decent judgment, the ability to build trust with the existing team, and the willingness to learn functions you didn't previously know. You have the baseline. The rest comes from getting in the room and figuring it out in real time.

*Variation C*
Operational work is something you can handle — it's not necessarily your favorite gear to operate in, but you've proven you can navigate complexity when required. A lot of high-operator-depth people overbuild and over-manage in the early days of ownership because they can, not because they should. Your profile suggests you'll be more selective about where you spend your operational energy, which can actually push you toward better delegation habits from day one.

*Variation D*
Your operational capability is solid — not a liability, not your dominant strength. That's actually where a lot of the best acquirers land. Pure operators sometimes struggle to let go. Pure investors sometimes get blindsided by the mess of a real business. You're somewhere in the middle, which means you can manage when you need to and delegate when you should. The key question for you isn't whether you can operate — it's whether you can build a team that can, and step back far enough to let them.

---

**HIGH (6.6 – 10.0)**

*Variation A*
Operator depth is one of your defining strengths. You know how to get into a business, assess what's broken, build a plan, and execute it. That's genuinely rare and genuinely valuable in the acquisition world — it's what lets you buy businesses others see as risky and turn them into assets. The watch-out at this level: strong operators sometimes over-invest in the operational layer and under-invest in the strategic layer. The goal isn't to be the best operator in the business — it's to build a business that operates without you.

*Variation B*
You're a genuine operator. You don't flinch at complexity, you know how to find the leverage points inside a business, and you can build and manage teams through uncertainty. This is the profile that acquires businesses other people pass on because they're "too messy" and creates real value where others saw risk. Just make sure your operating instinct doesn't keep you from building the systems and team that eventually free you from the day-to-day. Being the best operator in the room is only an advantage until you become the bottleneck.

*Variation C*
Your operational instinct is a serious asset. You understand how businesses actually work — not just on paper, but in the messy, human, unpredictable reality of day-to-day. That gives you an edge in acquisition evaluation (you can spot operational problems others miss), an edge in the early days of ownership (you know what to stabilize first), and an edge in building value over time (you know how to drive improvement where others would just watch). The discipline to develop at this level is knowing when to operate and when to step back and lead.

*Variation D*
Operations is where you live. You've done the reps, you understand systems and people and the thousand small things that make a business work, and you're not intimidated by the unpredictability of it. In the acquisition world, that's a genuine competitive advantage — it means you can buy businesses other buyers shy away from and create value through execution rather than just financial engineering. The next level for someone with your operational profile is usually about scaling beyond yourself: building the team, the systems, and the culture that lets the business run without requiring your daily presence.

### 7.3 CAPITAL READINESS

---

**LOW (1.0 – 3.5)**

*Variation A*
Capital readiness is where you have the most ground to cover before making a move. That's not unusual — most people who eventually buy a business weren't ready the first time they thought about it. What's useful to know is that the path to capital readiness is clearer than most people think. It's part savings, part education about deal structures (SBA financing can get you into a $1M+ business with roughly $100K down), and part building the track record that makes lenders and sellers want to work with you. You're building toward this.

*Variation B*
On the capital side, you're earlier in the journey than your other dimensions might suggest. The good news is that deal financing has democratized significantly — you don't need to show up with cash equal to the purchase price. SBA 7(a) loans, seller notes, and earnout structures have allowed people to buy businesses they couldn't have afforded on paper. What you do need is a working understanding of how those structures function and a clear-eyed plan for financing when the time comes. That's a knowledge problem, not just a capital problem.

*Variation C*
Capital readiness is your shortest plank right now. You have ground to cover here before a deal makes sense. The honest advice: don't let this hold you back from learning the acquisition process, understanding deal structures, and building your deal instinct in the meantime. The people who are ready to move fast when capital is in place are the ones who spent the prep period actually learning the game. Use this time well.

*Variation D*
Your capital position currently limits your ability to move on an acquisition, but this is almost certainly a temporary constraint rather than a permanent one. Understanding how deals are actually financed — most small business acquisitions use leverage, not just cash — is itself a form of capital readiness. Start there. Once you understand how to structure a deal, the amount of cash you actually need to close is often much lower than you'd assume.

---

**MEDIUM (3.6 – 6.5)**

*Variation A*
You're in reasonable financial shape and have some understanding of how acquisition financing works. The gap between here and being genuinely capital-ready is probably smaller than it feels. For most deals in the $500K–$2M range, you'd need roughly $100K–$200K in liquid capital plus the ability to qualify for an SBA loan. If you're approaching that zone, you're closer to deal-ready than not. The main thing to sharpen is your literacy on deal structures — how seller notes, earnouts, and SBA financing actually work in practice, not just in theory.

*Variation B*
Your capital position is developing. You're not starting from zero, and you understand that buying a business doesn't require buying it outright. What often holds people at your capital level back isn't the money itself — it's confidence about deploying it properly. Deal structure literacy tends to be the unlock: knowing the difference between an asset sale and a stock sale, how to think about working capital adjustments, what terms are standard vs. negotiable. The capital is often more accessible than people realize once the knowledge is there.

*Variation C*
You're in the middle of the capital readiness spectrum — enough to be serious, not yet positioned to close quickly if a deal required fast action. For most acquisition targets in the small business space, that's a manageable gap. The moves that close it fastest: getting clear on your exact capital position (not just a rough number), educating yourself on SBA pre-qualification, and understanding what deal structures are available to buyers at your stage. None of this is complicated — it's just work most aspiring acquirers haven't done yet.

*Variation D*
Financially, you're positioned to play — not at the top of the range, but you're in the game. You understand that leverage is part of the equation, and you've thought enough about your capital position to have a realistic sense of what you could deploy. At your level, the next step is usually getting specific: running a rough SBA pre-qualification, getting clear on your down payment capacity, and knowing what purchase price range you're actually shopping in. Vague capital readiness keeps people in research mode. Specific capital readiness moves them to action.

---

**HIGH (6.6 – 10.0)**

*Variation A*
Capital readiness is a clear strength. You're financially positioned to move on an acquisition and you understand how deal financing actually works — not just the concept but the mechanics. That combination is rarer than you'd think. A lot of aspiring buyers have either the capital or the knowledge, but not both. The risk for high-capital-ready buyers is sitting on that position too long waiting for the perfect deal. At some point, ready is ready. The opportunity cost of not deploying is real.

*Variation B*
You've done the financial groundwork. You know what you have, you understand how to structure a deal, and you're not relying on vague optimism that the money will work out. That's a serious advantage in a competitive acquisition process — sellers and brokers can tell when a buyer is financially prepared versus when they're stretching. Your capital position also means you have optionality: you can pursue a range of deal sizes and structures, which gives you a wider, better-quality funnel to work from.

*Variation C*
Strong capital readiness. You're in the position most aspiring acquirers are working toward — financially capable and educated on deal mechanics. What this buys you is speed and credibility in the deal process. When you find the right business, you can move decisively, and sellers will take you seriously. At this stage, the focus isn't on continuing to build capital readiness — it's on deploying it intelligently. The question shifts from "can I buy a business" to "which business is actually worth buying."

*Variation D*

High-capital-readiness buyers who end up with poor outcomes usually have the same problem: they deploy too quickly on a deal that wasn't right because they felt like they should be moving. Stay disciplined. The capital gives you patience. Use it.

### 7.4 RISK TOLERANCE
